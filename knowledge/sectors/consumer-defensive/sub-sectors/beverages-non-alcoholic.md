# Beverages - Non-Alcoholic

## Overview

The US non-alcoholic beverages market was valued at ~$280B in 2023 (projected $457B by 2030, 7.4% CAGR). The industry is dominated by Coca-Cola (KO) and PepsiCo (PEP) with combined ~70% carbonated soft drink market share. Growth segments include energy drinks ($24B, 8.1% CAGR — led by Monster MNST and Celsius CELH), bottled water ($10.1B), and functional beverages. The industry operates an asset-light franchise/bottling model (KO, PEP) alongside vertically integrated manufacturers (Monster, Celsius).

Capital intensity varies dramatically. Concentrate companies (KO) operate at 60%+ gross margins and 30%+ EBITDA margins with minimal capex. Bottler-distributors run at 35-40% gross margins, 12-18% EBITDA margins. Energy drink disruptors (Celsius) can achieve 50%+ gross margins via co-manufacturing. FCF is highly stable for incumbents; newer entrants show cyclicality tied to growth investment.

Barriers to entry are extremely high: shelf-space access, distribution scale, marketing spend ($4B+ annually for KO), and brand loyalty. The franchise bottler system is a structural moat. However, DSD (Direct Store Delivery) model allows disruptors to enter via convenience/gas station channels.

## Valuation Approach

- **Primary method:** P/E and EV/EBITDA — industry convention for stable consumer businesses
- **Secondary method:** P/FCF, DCF for growth stories (Celsius, Monster)
- **Why FCF multiples work:** Stable, predictable cash flows make FCF multiples reliable. Baseline 22-28x for established brands (KO, PEP); premium multiples 30-35x for high-growth (CELH at peak)
- **Typical EV/EBITDA range:** 16-22x for large-cap (KO, PEP); 20-30x for high-growth (MNST, CELH); 10-14x for commodity/private label
- **Common pitfalls:** Extrapolating hyper-growth in energy drinks (CELH went from 60x to 25x P/E); ignoring FX impact on multinationals (KO earns ~65% internationally); conflating revenue growth with volume growth (pricing can mask demand decline)

## Turnaround Precedents

| Company | Period | Situation | Actions Taken | Outcome | Timeline | Stock Recovery |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------------|---------|----------|----------------|
| Dr Pepper Snapple / Keurig (KDP) | 2018-2021 | Post-merger integration challenges, heavy debt from Keurig acquisition (~$16B), coffee segment slowing | Aggressive deleveraging, portfolio rationalization, cost synergies, pricing discipline | Success — debt reduced, consistent earnings growth | 3 years | ~$23 to $35+ |
| SodaStream (pre-PEP) | 2014-2016 | Revenue decline as home carbonation fad faded, stock fell 70% from peak | Pivoted to sparkling water positioning, international expansion, retail partnerships | Partial success — acquired by PEP for $3.2B in 2018 | 2-3 years | ~$22 to $143 (acquisition) |
| Cott Corporation | 2014-2018 | Private label beverage volume decline, margin compression, commodity competition | Pivoted to water/coffee services (acquired S&D Coffee, DS Services), exited private label bottling | Partial — renamed Primo Water, became water delivery leader | 4 years | ~$6 to $15 |

**Pattern:** 2 of 3 cases achieved meaningful recovery within 3 years -- ~67% base rate. Successful turnarounds required decisive portfolio pivots (exit declining categories) and either M&A-driven repositioning or aggressive deleveraging. Companies stuck in commodity/private label without differentiation tend to fade rather than recover.

## Risk Profile

- **Default risk type:** Operational / Financial
- **Cyclicality:** Low — beverage consumption is recession-resistant; slight mix shift to value during downturns
- **Regulatory discretion:** Low-Moderate — FDA regulates labeling and ingredients but enforcement is predictable
- **Macro sensitivity:** Inflation (Moderate — sugar, PET, aluminum costs), FX (Moderate for multinationals), Consumer income (Low), Trade policy (Low-Moderate)
- **Binary trigger frequency:** Rare

### Kill Factors (hard reject)

1. **Major contamination event destroying brand trust** — widespread recall of core product with health consequences. Historical: no major US soft drink contamination in recent decades, but it would be devastating.
2. **Loss of franchise bottler network** — for concentrate companies, losing key bottling partners strands distribution.
3. **Regulatory ban on core ingredient** — e.g., if FDA banned a key energy drink ingredient (unlikely but binary).

### Friction Factors (PCS modifiers)

1. **Sugar tax expansion to federal level** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: would structurally reduce volumes in core CSD category
2. **Single-brand/category concentration > 60%** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: category-specific risk (e.g., energy drinks if regulatory scrutiny increases)
3. **FX exposure > 50% of revenue** — suggested friction: 0 to -1, rationale: currency headwinds can mask/amplify underlying performance for years

## Thesis-Breaking Patterns

- **Failure mode 1:** Growth category collapse — company bets heavily on a hot category that fades (hard seltzer, kombucha). Gradual. Examples: numerous small-cap beverage companies 2020-2023.
- **Failure mode 2:** Private label erosion — retailer brands capture share from branded products during prolonged inflation. Gradual. Examples: store-brand water gaining share from Dasani/Aquafina.
- **Failure mode 3:** Overleveraged M&A — debt-funded acquisition fails to deliver synergies. Gradual. Examples: Cott pre-pivot struggled with commodity bottling economics.

### Red Flags If Missing

- If volume/price split is not disclosed, cannot distinguish real demand from pricing-driven revenue
- If category share data is unavailable, competitive assessment relies solely on total revenue trends
