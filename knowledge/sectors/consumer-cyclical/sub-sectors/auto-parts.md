# Auto Parts

## Overview

The US auto parts industry divides into two fundamentally different businesses: OEM suppliers (manufacturing components for vehicle assembly) and aftermarket distributors/retailers (replacement parts for vehicles already on the road). OEM suppliers like Cooper-Standard (CPS) operate in a capital-intensive, cyclical business with thin margins (EBITDA ~6-10%), heavy R&D spending, and customer concentration risk tied to a handful of automakers. Aftermarket players split further into retailers (AAP Advance Auto Parts, AZO AutoZone, ORLY O'Reilly) serving DIY/DIFM channels, and parts manufacturers like Dorman Products (DORM) that design and distribute replacement parts.

The US light-duty aftermarket is projected at over $400 billion in 2024, growing at ~6% CAGR through 2026 ([Source: indexbox.io](https://vertexaisearch.cloud.google.com/grounding-api-redirect/AUZIYQHkFElNPHLwoTB6wLI4jQAI5ujJDzUu41xhOLzLSk0ryzCRy0MiMZ_95SMiliKP_e1S8qrqwKF8eHcubpUHCnLvsoKVH4_7g--58zIEcRypa3wHT1Fq0jBebabEhLxf1ShVd8clga0wMmLJEbt05eOmdcYcfdDcxWeuAGPk7MUpn9ACpJ0D0EmcumBV__Y=)). The aftermarket benefits from a structural tailwind: the average US vehicle age reached 12.8 years in 2025, driving replacement demand. OEM parts manufacturing is highly capital-intensive, with projects of $1 billion+ now accounting for 43% of automotive capital spending (up from 18% a decade ago). Aftermarket retail/distribution is less capital-intensive but requires massive inventory investment and extensive distribution networks.

Key competitive dynamics: OEM parts guarantee perfect fit and carry manufacturer warranties but cost more. Aftermarket parts are 30-60% cheaper, with Chinese imports increasingly disrupting pricing. The EV transition threatens OEM suppliers of ICE-specific components (exhaust, fuel systems, transmissions) while aftermarket demand for traditional maintenance parts remains resilient as the existing ICE fleet ages.

## Valuation Approach

- **Primary method (OEM suppliers):** EV/EBITDA -- mean 9.0x, median 8.5x (Q3 2025 data). Distressed OEM suppliers may trade at 4-6x.
- **Primary method (Aftermarket retailers):** EV/EBITDA or P/FCF. AZO trades at ~20x EV/EBITDA, ORLY at ~26x. P/FCF: AZO ~32x, ORLY ~35x, DORM ~23x (as of March 2026).
- **Primary method (Aftermarket parts manufacturers):** EV/EBITDA 12-16x for quality names like DORM; P/FCF 18-25x.
- **Why the OEM/Aftermarket split matters:** OEM suppliers deserve cyclical multiples (8-12x EV/EBITDA) due to customer concentration, capital intensity, and OEM production cyclicality. Aftermarket retailers deserve consumer staples-like multiples (18-25x FCF) because replacement parts demand is largely non-discretionary and counter-cyclical.
- **Distressed OEM valuation:** When OEM suppliers are in distress (CPS-type situations), standard EV/EBITDA breaks down because EBITDA may be negative. Use EV/Revenue (0.3-0.6x for distressed) or asset-based valuation (liquidation value of manufacturing plants).
- **Common pitfalls:** (1) Applying aftermarket retailer multiples to OEM suppliers. (2) Ignoring the structural difference between parts manufacturers (DORM) and parts retailers (AAP). (3) Not adjusting for debt loads -- many OEM suppliers carry significant leverage post-restructuring.

## Turnaround Precedents

| Company | Period | Situation | Actions Taken | Outcome | Timeline | Stock Recovery |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------------|---------|----------|----------------|
| Dana Holdings | 2006-2010 | Chapter 11 (Feb 2008), $2.7B debt, revenue decline from auto downturn | Debt restructuring via Ch.11, shed unprofitable divisions, emerged Mar 2008, relisted | Successful -- emerged profitable, rebuilt as diversified supplier | ~2 years in Ch.11, 3yr full recovery | Relisted NYSE 2008, recovered to pre-crisis levels by 2013 |
| Federal-Mogul | 2001-2007 | Chapter 11 (Oct 2001), $4.3B liabilities including asbestos, declining margins | Asbestos trust, debt restructuring, operational improvements | Partial -- emerged 2007 but never regained prior trajectory | 6 years in bankruptcy | Emerged 2007, acquired by Icahn 2012, taken private |
| Cooper-Standard | 2010-2014 | Chapter 11 (Aug 2009), GFC-driven auto production collapse, $1B+ debt | Debt elimination via Ch.11, emerged May 2010, refocused on sealing/fuel/brake systems | Successful -- emerged lean, grew revenue and margins | 9 months in Ch.11, 4yr growth period | IPO'd 2011, rose from ~$20 to $120 by 2018 |
| Advance Auto Parts | 2023-present | Operating margin collapsed from ~9% to <4%, negative FCF, CEO replaced Sept 2023 | New CEO Shane O'Kelly from HD Supply, store optimization, SKU rationalization, Worldpac divestiture | In progress -- early signs of margin improvement, sold Worldpac for $1.5B | Ongoing, 2+ years into restructuring | Stock declined from ~$200 (2022) to ~$30 (2025), partial recovery pending |
| Delphi Technologies | 2018-2020 | Revenue declined 15%+, negative FCF, EV transition pressure, high leverage | Attempted standalone restructuring, ultimately acquired by BorgWarner Jan 2020 ($3.3B all-stock) | Acquisition -- could not execute standalone turnaround | ~2 years of decline before acquisition | Stock declined ~60% before acquisition premium |

**Pattern:** Successful auto parts turnarounds typically require (1) debt restructuring (often via Chapter 11 for OEM suppliers), (2) operational simplification (shedding non-core divisions), and (3) a multi-year demand recovery cycle. OEM supplier turnarounds take 2-4 years minimum. Aftermarket retail turnarounds (like AAP) require new management and take 3-5 years. 3 of 5 cases above achieved meaningful recovery -- base rate ~60%, though only 2 of 5 achieved full stock recovery.

## Risk Profile

- **Default risk type:** Cyclical (OEM suppliers) / Operational (Aftermarket retailers)
- **Cyclicality:** High (OEM suppliers tied to vehicle production volumes) / Moderate (Aftermarket -- counter-cyclical to new vehicle sales)
- **Regulatory discretion:** Moderate -- NHTSA recalls are rules-based but enforcement timing is discretionary
- **Macro sensitivity:**
  - Interest rates: High (drives vehicle purchase/hold decisions, directly affects aftermarket demand)
  - Consumer confidence: High (DIY spending is somewhat discretionary)
  - Trade policy/tariffs: High (25% tariffs on imported parts effective May 2025, 50% on steel/aluminum)
  - EV transition: High (OEM suppliers), Low (aftermarket retailers for next 10+ years)
  - Unemployment: Moderate (affects professional installer demand)
- **Binary trigger frequency:** Occasional (recalls, OEM platform losses, tariff shocks)

### Kill Factors (hard reject -- maps to enrichment override triggers)

1. **100% ICE-component OEM supplier with no EV pivot** -- EV transition makes entire product line obsolete within 10-15 years. Example: suppliers of exhaust systems, fuel tanks, multi-speed transmissions. If >80% revenue from components that EVs eliminate, thesis is structurally broken.

2. **Single OEM customer >50% of revenue AND that OEM is in financial distress** -- Visteon's near-total dependence on Ford (pre-bankruptcy) shows how OEM supplier concentration becomes fatal when the customer is impaired.

3. **Debt covenant violation with no clear path to waiver or restructuring** -- First Brands Group (Sept 2025) showed how a failed $6B refinancing triggered catastrophic liquidity shortfall. Liabilities $10-50B vs assets $1-10B.

4. **Active Takata-scale product recall liability** -- recall costs approaching $25B destroyed Takata entirely. Any supplier facing recall liability exceeding 2x annual revenue with no insurance coverage is uninvestable.

### Friction Factors (PCS modifiers -- reduce confidence, don't kill thesis)

1. **Tariff exposure on imported components >30% of COGS** -- suggested friction: -1. Rationale: 25% tariff on auto parts (May 2025) directly compresses margins. Manageable if company can pass through or re-source, but creates multi-quarter uncertainty.

2. **CEO tenure <1 year during active turnaround** -- suggested friction: -1. Rationale: AAP's restructuring under new CEO (Sept 2023) shows execution is uncertain in early stages. Probability ceiling also applies (65%).

3. **OEM production volume sensitivity >70%** -- suggested friction: -1. Rationale: Cyclical exposure means thesis depends on macro conditions outside management's control.

4. **Chinese aftermarket parts competitive pressure** -- suggested friction: 0 to -1. Rationale: Chinese parts priced 30-60% below American counterparts and expanding. Affects pricing power but not existential for established brands.

5. **Active litigation / product liability claims** -- suggested friction: -1 to -2. Rationale: Recall-related lawsuits can escalate. Share of recall costs borne by suppliers has tripled from 5-7% to 15-20%.

## Thesis-Breaking Patterns

- **Failure mode 1: Secular demand destruction from EV transition** -- gradual (10-15 year horizon) but accelerating. Companies with 100% ICE revenue face slow death. Examples: Delphi Technologies (pre-BorgWarner acquisition), legacy exhaust system manufacturers.

- **Failure mode 2: Debt spiral from leveraged buyout/restructuring** -- binary when refinancing fails. Private equity-owned auto suppliers frequently carry unsustainable leverage. Example: Tenneco (acquired by Apollo 2022, ~$4.2B debt, B-/negative outlook), First Brands Group (Chapter 11, Sept 2025, $6B on-balance-sheet + $4B off-balance-sheet debt).

- **Failure mode 3: OEM concentration + production downturn** -- gradual to binary. Revenue collapses when key OEM customer cuts production. Example: Visteon bankruptcy (May 2009) from Ford dependence during GFC.

- **Failure mode 4: Aftermarket execution failure** -- gradual. Poor inventory management, wrong store locations, weak DIFM strategy. Example: AAP's margin collapse 2019-2024, declining from 9% operating margin to <4%.

### Red Flags If Missing

- If debt maturity schedule is not disclosed for a leveraged OEM supplier, analyst cannot assess refinancing risk
- If customer concentration is not broken out, analyst cannot evaluate OEM platform loss risk
- If ICE/EV revenue split is unavailable for an OEM supplier, transition risk is unquantifiable

## Epistemic Profile

| PCS Question | Typical Answer | Rationale |
|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| Q1: Operational risk? | Depends on sub-type | OEM suppliers: Yes (manufacturing execution). Aftermarket retailers: Yes (store operations, inventory). |
| Q2: Regulatory minimal? | Yes (mostly) | NHTSA recalls are rules-based. No discretionary licensing. Tariffs are the main regulatory risk but are binary policy decisions, not ongoing discretion. |
| Q3: Historical precedent? | Yes | Multiple turnaround precedents exist: Dana, Cooper-Standard, Federal-Mogul, AAP (in progress). Base rate ~60% recovery. |
| Q4: Non-binary outcome? | Yes (usually) | Gradient outcomes typical for aftermarket. OEM suppliers can face binary events (covenant violation, recall liability) but usually have workout options. |
| Q5: Macro/geo limited? | No | Significant exposure to interest rates, tariffs, EV transition, and auto production cycles. |

**Epistemic stability:** 3/5 -- Aftermarket retail is relatively stable (non-discretionary demand), but OEM suppliers face multiple structural uncertainties (EV transition, tariffs, production cycles). The mix of sub-types within "Auto Parts" makes generalization difficult.

**Suggested PCS friction:** -1 -- Cyclical/macro exposure (tariffs, interest rates, EV transition) adds structural uncertainty beyond pure operational risk.
