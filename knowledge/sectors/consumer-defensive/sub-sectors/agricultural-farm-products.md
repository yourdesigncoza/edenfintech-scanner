# Agricultural Farm Products

## Overview

US agricultural products companies include Archer-Daniels-Midland (ADM, FY2024 revenue ~$85.5B), Bunge Global (BG, ~$53B), Corteva Agriscience (CTVA, ~$17B — seeds/crop protection), FMC Corporation (FMC, ~$4.5B — crop chemicals), Dole plc (DOLE, ~$8B — fresh produce), and Fresh Del Monte Produce (FDP, ~$4.2B). Revenue comes from commodity processing (oilseeds, grains), food ingredient manufacturing, agricultural inputs (seeds, chemicals), and fresh produce distribution.

Capital intensity is high. Commodity processors require massive grain elevators, processing plants, transportation infrastructure (river barges, rail), and working capital for commodity purchases. Gross margins are thin for commodity processors: ADM ~8-10%, Bunge ~8-9%. Crop science companies (CTVA) have higher margins: 40-45% gross, ~20% EBITDA. FCF is highly cyclical, driven by commodity price cycles, crop yields, and trade flows.

The sector is an oligopoly at the processing level — the "ABCD" companies (ADM, Bunge, Cargill-private, Louis Dreyfus-private) control ~70% of global grain trade. Barriers to entry are enormous: infrastructure (elevators, ports, processing plants), global logistics networks, commodity risk management expertise, and regulatory relationships.

## Valuation Approach

- **Primary method:** EV/EBITDA using mid-cycle earnings — critical because commodity processors' earnings swing 50%+ through cycles
- **Secondary method:** P/E on normalized/mid-cycle earnings; P/B for capital-intensive processors
- **Why deep discount from sector baseline:** Commodity cyclicality, thin margins, and trade policy risk justify 8-14x FCF. This is a commodity processing business, not a branded consumer business.
- **Typical EV/EBITDA range:** 7-10x for commodity processors (ADM, BG) on mid-cycle; 12-16x for crop science (CTVA); 6-8x for fresh produce (DOLE, FDP)
- **Common pitfalls:** Valuing at peak-cycle earnings (commodity prices mean-revert); ignoring working capital's impact on FCF; using trailing multiples in a cyclical business; not accounting for trade policy risk in forward estimates

|---------|--------|-----------|--------------|---------|----------|----------------|
| ADM | 1995-2000 | Price-fixing scandal (lysine cartel), $100M fine, three executives imprisoned, credibility destroyed | New management, compliance overhaul, operational restructuring, diversification into nutrition ingredients | Success — survived and eventually thrived | 5 years | ~$10 to $15 (modest, then long bull run) |
| ADM | 2024-present | Accounting irregularities in Nutrition segment, CFO placed on leave, SEC/DOJ investigation, stock fell ~30% | Investigation ongoing, restated some results, new CFO appointed | Ongoing — uncertain outcome | TBD | ~$60 to $45 (still declining) |
| Bunge | 2017-2020 | Earnings decline from weak crush margins, activist pressure (D.E. | FMC Corp | 2023-present | Revenue decline as crop chemical demand normalized post-pandemic, destocking by distributors, stock fell 60% | Cost restructuring, new product launches (diamide insecticides), inventory management | Ongoing — stabilizing but not yet recovered | TBD | ~$130 to $50 |

**Pattern:** 2 of 4 completed cases recovered -- ~50% base rate but long timelines (3-5 years). Commodity businesses recover when the cycle turns — the company just needs to survive. Accounting scandals (ADM 1995, ADM 2024) create extended uncertainty. Key differentiator: scale advantage (oligopoly position) provides floor; companies with diversification beyond pure commodity processing recover faster.

## Risk Profile

- **Default risk type:** Cyclical / Macro
- **Cyclicality:** High — commodity price cycles drive earnings volatility by 50%+ peak-to-trough
- **Regulatory discretion:** Moderate — USDA, EPA (pesticide regulation), CFTC (commodity trading oversight)
- **Macro sensitivity:** Commodity prices (EXTREME), Trade policy/tariffs (EXTREME — China trade war directly impacts grain exports), Weather/crop yields (High), FX/emerging market currencies (High), Biofuel mandates (Moderate)
- **Binary trigger frequency:** Occasional — trade embargoes, accounting fraud, regulatory action

### Kill Factors (hard reject)

1. **Accounting fraud/SEC enforcement leading to delisting** — ADM's 2024 accounting issues demonstrate this is a live risk. If fraud is systemic, company credibility is destroyed.
2. **Trade embargo cutting off major export market** — if China bans US grain imports (as partially done in 2018-2019 trade war), commodity processors face immediate demand destruction.
3. **Environmental catastrophe from operations** — chemical spill, grain elevator explosion causing deaths and regulatory shutdown.

### Friction Factors (PCS modifiers)

1. **Trade war/tariff escalation** — suggested friction: -1 to -2, rationale: agricultural exports are directly targeted in trade disputes; China tariffs cut US soybean exports by 75% in 2018
2. **Commodity cycle at peak** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: peak earnings multiples look cheap but earnings will mean-revert; buy-the-dip timing risk
3. **Accounting investigation** — suggested friction: -2, rationale: ongoing SEC/DOJ investigation creates binary legal risk with unpredictable timeline (ADM 2024)
4. **Weather/climate risk concentration** — suggested friction: 0 to -1, rationale: crop failures from drought/flooding can disrupt processing volumes and commodity pricing

## Thesis-Breaking Patterns

- **Failure mode 1:** Trade war demand destruction — retaliatory tariffs shut off key export markets. Binary trigger, gradual impact. Example: US-China soybean tariffs 2018 (exports fell 75%).
- **Failure mode 2:** Accounting fraud/compliance failure — systemic fraud destroys credibility and invites regulatory action. Binary. Example: ADM lysine price-fixing (1995), ADM Nutrition accounting (2024).
- **Failure mode 3:** Commodity cycle downturn with leverage — if company expanded at cycle peak with debt, downturn creates solvency risk. Gradual. Example: Many agricultural companies during 2014-2016 commodity downturn.
- **Failure mode 4:** Crop chemical regulatory ban — EPA bans a key product. Binary. Example: Chlorpyrifos ban discussions affecting FMC/CTVA.

- If export concentration by country is not disclosed, trade policy risk cannot be sized

## Epistemic Profile

| PCS Question | Typical Answer | Rationale |
|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| Q1: Operational risk? | No | Dominant risk is macro (commodity cycles, trade policy), not operational |
| Q2: Regulatory minimal? | Mostly | USDA/EPA are generally rules-based; CFTC enforcement is predictable |
| Q3: Historical precedent? | Moderate | Commodity cycle turnarounds have precedent; accounting scandals are harder to predict |
| Q4: Non-binary outcome? | Mostly | Commodity cycles create gradual swings; trade embargoes and fraud are more binary |
| Q5: Macro/geo limited? | No | Extreme exposure to commodity prices, trade policy, FX, and weather |

**Epistemic stability:** 2/5 -- Business model is simple (buy/process/sell commodities) but outcomes are dominated by external factors (commodity cycles, trade policy, weather) that are fundamentally hard to predict. Accounting risk adds another layer of uncertainty. Mid-cycle valuation is the best approach but cycle timing is notoriously difficult.
**Suggested PCS friction:** -1 -- Cyclical/macro risk dominates this sub-sector. Apply -2 for companies with active accounting investigations or concentrated trade policy exposure.
