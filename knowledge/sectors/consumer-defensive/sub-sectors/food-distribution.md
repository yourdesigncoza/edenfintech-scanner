# Food Distribution

## Overview

US food distribution is dominated by three companies: Sysco (SYY, FY2024 revenue ~$78B), US Foods (USFD, ~$37B), and Performance Food Group (PFGC, ~$58B). Smaller players include UNFI (UNFI, ~$31B — natural/organic focus, SuperValu legacy) and SpartanNash (SPTN, ~$9B — hybrid distributor/retailer). Revenue comes from distributing food and related products to restaurants (~65% of industry volume), healthcare facilities, schools, grocery stores, and other foodservice operators.

Capital intensity is high. Distributors require massive cold-chain infrastructure (warehouses, refrigerated trucks, fleet management), technology for order management, and working capital for inventory. Gross margins are thin: 16-20% (SYY ~19%, USFD ~19%, PFGC ~12%). EBITDA margins: 4-7% (SYY ~6.5%, USFD ~5.5%, PFGC ~3.5%). FCF is moderate and somewhat cyclical, driven by restaurant spending and fuel costs.

Competition is scale-driven. The Big Three control ~35% of the $350B+ broadline distribution market. Barriers to entry are significant: cold-chain logistics infrastructure, customer relationships, purchasing scale (volume rebates from manufacturers), and geographic density of delivery routes. However, the industry remains fragmented with thousands of local distributors.

## Valuation Approach

- **Primary method:** EV/EBITDA — standard for food distribution
- **Secondary method:** P/E; EV/gross profit for comparing companies with different cost structures
- **Why discount from sector baseline:** Thin margins, capital intensity, and fuel cost exposure justify 12-18x FCF. This is a logistics business with consumer staples customers, not a consumer staples brand business.
- **Typical EV/EBITDA range:** 12-16x for Sysco (market leader premium); 10-14x for US Foods/PFGC; 6-8x for UNFI (distressed/leveraged); 7-10x for SpartanNash
- **Common pitfalls:** Comparing food distributor multiples to food brand companies (completely different economics); ignoring fuel cost exposure in margin analysis; not adjusting for acquired vs organic growth; underestimating customer concentration risk in local markets

## Turnaround Precedents

| Company | Period | Situation | Actions Taken | Outcome | Timeline | Stock Recovery |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------------|---------|----------|----------------|
| UNFI (UNFI) | 2019-2023 | Overleveraged from $2.9B SuperValu acquisition (2018), integration challenges, COVID supply chain disruption, net debt/EBITDA > 5x | Debt reduction, operational efficiency programs, divested non-core assets, renegotiated customer contracts | Partial — debt reduced but still challenged, stock well below pre-acquisition levels | 4+ years | ~$10 to $20 range (from $70+ pre-acquisition) |
| Sysco (SYY) | 2020-2022 | COVID-19 destroyed 65% of restaurant foodservice demand overnight, revenue fell 20%+ | Cost restructuring, retained customer relationships, acquisitioned smaller distributors at distress prices, recovered with restaurant reopening | Success — revenue recovered to pre-COVID levels, used crisis to consolidate market | 2 years | ~$40 to $85+ |
| Performance Food Group (PFGC) | 2020-2023 | COVID foodservice collapse, plus integration of $2.3B Core-Mark acquisition (2021) | Aggressive M&A (Core-Mark added convenience store distribution), cost management, pricing discipline | Success — diversified beyond restaurants, strong growth | 3 years | ~$25 to $70+ |

**Pattern:** 2 of 3 achieved recovery -- ~67% base rate. Successful turnarounds required market leader position (Sysco's scale advantage), operational discipline, and using downturns to consolidate. UNFI's cautionary tale: overleveraged M&A in a thin-margin business creates a multi-year debt trap. COVID was an extreme exogenous shock that paradoxically helped strong operators gain share.

## Risk Profile

- **Default risk type:** Operational / Cyclical
- **Cyclicality:** Moderate — foodservice demand correlates with restaurant spending and consumer confidence
- **Regulatory discretion:** Low — FDA FSMA compliance is rules-based; DOT fleet regulations are predictable
- **Macro sensitivity:** Restaurant spending (High), Fuel costs (Moderate), Food inflation (Moderate — pass-through with lag), Labor costs/driver shortage (Moderate), Supply chain disruptions (Moderate)
- **Binary trigger frequency:** Rare — COVID-type demand shock is the main binary scenario

### Kill Factors (hard reject)

1. **Pandemic-level demand destruction** — if restaurant closures recur at 2020 levels, smaller distributors face immediate liquidity crisis
2. **Major food safety/contamination from distribution chain** — if a distributor's cold chain fails and causes widespread foodborne illness
3. **Overleveraged M&A without integration path** — thin-margin businesses cannot service 5x+ EBITDA debt. Example: UNFI/SuperValu nearly became an LBO-style failure.

### Friction Factors (PCS modifiers)

1. **Customer concentration in restaurant chains** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: if a major restaurant chain switches distributor or goes bankrupt, impact is concentrated
2. **Fuel cost spike** — suggested friction: 0 to -1, rationale: diesel is 1.5-2.5% of revenue; $1/gallon increase can compress margins 50-100bps
3. **M&A integration in thin-margin business** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: history shows food distribution M&A has high failure rate when leverage exceeds 4x

## Thesis-Breaking Patterns

- **Failure mode 1:** Overleveraged M&A — acquiring at 10-12x EBITDA in a 5-7% margin business creates a debt trap. Gradual. Example: UNFI/SuperValu.
- **Failure mode 2:** Exogenous demand shock — pandemic, recession-driven restaurant closures. Binary. Example: COVID-19 (2020).
- **Failure mode 3:** Customer loss cascading — losing a major national account (restaurant chain) forces route density decline and cost per delivery increase. Gradual.

| Q2: Regulatory minimal? | Yes | FDA FSMA and DOT regulations are rules-based and predictable |
| Q3: Historical precedent? | Moderate | COVID provided unique precedent; M&A failures (UNFI) are instructive |
