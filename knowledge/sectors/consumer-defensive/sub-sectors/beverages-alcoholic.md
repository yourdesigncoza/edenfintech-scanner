# Beverages - Alcoholic (Brewers & Beer)

## Overview

The US alcoholic beverages industry (brewers) generated approximately $129.2B in 2025 revenue. The market is dominated by two global players — Anheuser-Busch InBev (BUD) and Molson Coors (TAP) — alongside mid-cap operators like Boston Beer (SAM) and Constellation Brands (STZ). Revenue comes primarily from branded beer sales, with increasing diversification into "Beyond Beer" categories (hard seltzers, RTDs, spirits). Boston Beer derives ~85% of volume from Beyond Beer (Twisted Tea, Truly). Premiumization is the key growth driver as volume declines in core beer.

Capital intensity is moderate. Brewers require significant investment in brewing facilities, distribution infrastructure, and marketing. Gross margins range 50-60% for premium brands, with EBITDA margins of 25-35% for large-cap brewers. FCF generation is relatively stable given inelastic demand, though craft brewers face higher volatility. The three-tier distribution system (brewer-distributor-retailer) creates structural barriers to entry and makes brand-building expensive.

Competitive dynamics are concentrated: BUD holds ~40% US beer market share, Molson Coors ~25%. Craft brewers collectively hold ~13% by volume but face consolidation pressure. Barriers to entry include distribution network access, brand investment requirements ($50M+ for national launch), and regulatory licensing.

## Valuation Approach

- **Primary method:** EV/EBITDA — standard for beverages given varying capital structures and tax rates
- **Secondary method:** P/E, P/FCF for stable-state companies; DCF for growth stories
- **Why NOT pure FCF multiples:** FCF multiples work but EV/EBITDA is the industry convention due to M&A comparability. Baseline FCF multiple: 20-25x for established brands (Consumer Staples baseline 25-28x, modest discount for volume declines)
- **Typical EV/EBITDA range:** 10-14x for large-cap brewers; 15-20x for premium/craft with growth; 8-10x for commodity/declining
- **Common pitfalls:** Overpaying for "Beyond Beer" growth (Truly hard seltzer collapsed from peak); ignoring distributor economics; treating promotional volume as sustainable

## Turnaround Precedents

| Company | Period | Situation | Actions Taken | Outcome | Timeline | Stock Recovery |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------------|---------|----------|----------------|
| Boston Beer (SAM) | 2016-2018 | Craft beer slowdown, market share loss, Samuel Adams volume decline | Pivoted to Truly Hard Seltzer, Twisted Tea expansion, cost restructuring | Success — Truly became #2 hard seltzer, revenue doubled | 2 years | ~$130 to $1,000+ (2016-2021 peak) |
| Molson Coors (TAP) | 2019-2023 | Declining premium light beer volumes, Bud Light controversy competitor | Invested in Coors Light/Miller Lite marketing, acquired craft brands, Blue Moon expansion | Partial success — gained market share from Bud Light crisis (2023) | 4 years | ~$45 to $65 |
| Constellation Brands (STZ) | 2018-2020 | Canopy Growth cannabis investment write-down (~$800M loss), wine/spirits portfolio underperformance | Divested low-end wine brands, focused on Modelo/Corona (beer), unwound cannabis exposure | Success — Modelo became #1 US beer brand by 2023 | 3 years | ~$150 to $250+ |

**Pattern:** 3 of 3 cases recovered within 2-4 years -- ~100% base rate but sample is small and survivorship-biased. Successful turnarounds required portfolio pivot to growth categories and willingness to divest underperformers. Key differentiator: companies with strong brands in growing sub-categories (Mexican imports, hard tea, seltzer) recovered; those dependent on declining core (mainstream US beer) struggled.

## Risk Profile

- **Default risk type:** Operational / Financial
- **Cyclicality:** Low — alcohol consumption is relatively recession-resistant
- **Regulatory discretion:** Moderate — TTB licensing, state-by-state distribution laws
- **Macro sensitivity:** Inflation (Low-Moderate), Consumer income (Low), Trade policy/tariffs (Moderate for imports), Commodity costs — aluminum, barley, hops (Moderate)
- **Binary trigger frequency:** Rare

### Kill Factors (hard reject -- maps to enrichment override triggers)

1. **Loss of distribution rights in major markets** — three-tier system means losing a key distributor can strand 20-30% of volume overnight. Example: craft brewers forced to self-distribute after distributor consolidation.
2. **Contamination/safety recall affecting core brand** — while rare, a widespread contamination event could destroy brand trust. No major historical example in US beer.
3. **Debt spiral from failed acquisition** — overleveraged M&A (net debt/EBITDA > 6x) with no clear deleveraging path.

### Friction Factors (PCS modifiers -- reduce confidence, don't kill thesis)

1. **Secular volume decline in core category** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: US beer volume declining ~1-2%/year requires successful premiumization or category diversification to offset
2. **Single brand concentration > 50% of revenue** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: brand-specific risk (see Bud Light 2023 controversy wiping ~25% of brand volume)
3. **Trade policy uncertainty on imported inputs** — suggested friction: 0 to -1, rationale: aluminum tariffs, barley imports could compress margins

## Thesis-Breaking Patterns

- **Failure mode 1:** Failed "Beyond Beer" pivot — company invests heavily in new category (e.g., hard seltzer) that collapses. Gradual. Examples: Truly volume decline 2022-2023 after seltzer bust.
- **Failure mode 2:** Brand crisis / consumer boycott — sudden loss of brand loyalty from controversy. Binary. Examples: Bud Light 2023 lost ~25% volume in months.
- **Failure mode 3:** Overleveraged acquisition — taking on 5x+ debt for an acquisition that doesn't deliver synergies. Gradual. Examples: Molson Coors post-2016 merger struggled with debt reduction for years.
