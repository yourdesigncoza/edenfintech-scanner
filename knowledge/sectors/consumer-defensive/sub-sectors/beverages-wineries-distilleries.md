# Beverages - Wineries & Distilleries

## Overview

The US wine market generates ~$105B in retail value; spirits ~$40B+ wholesale. NYSE-listed companies include Brown-Forman (BF.B — Jack Daniel's, Woodford Reserve), Diageo (DEO — Johnnie Walker, Crown Royal, Smirnoff), Duckhorn Portfolio (NAPA), MGP Ingredients (MGPI), and The Duckhorn Portfolio. Revenue models differ significantly: wine emphasizes DTC (tasting rooms, wine clubs — 50%+ margins vs 20-30% wholesale) while spirits rely on wholesale distribution with brand premiumization.

Capital intensity is high. Wineries require vineyard land, aging facilities, and barrel inventory (3-10 year aging for premium wine/spirits). Distilleries face similar aging requirements — brown spirits (bourbon, whiskey) tie up working capital for 4-12+ years. Gross margins range 50-70% for premium spirits (Brown-Forman ~60%), 40-55% for premium wine, and 20-30% for bulk wine/spirits producers. EBITDA margins: 30-40% for premium spirits brands, 15-25% for wine.

Barriers to entry are high for premium segments: brand heritage (Jack Daniel's has 150+ years), aging inventory (years of capital lock-up), distribution relationships, and vineyard terroir. Low-end wine/spirits face lower barriers. The three-tier distribution system constrains DTC expansion in many states.

## Valuation Approach

- **Primary method:** EV/EBITDA — standard for spirits and wine companies
- **Secondary method:** P/E for stable dividend payers (BF.B); DCF adjusting for aging inventory working capital
- **Why discounted from sector baseline:** Significant working capital tie-up in aging inventory reduces FCF quality. Baseline FCF multiple 15-22x (below Consumer Staples 25-28x due to capital intensity and inventory risk)
- **Typical EV/EBITDA range:** 15-20x for premium spirits (BF.B); 10-14x for wine producers; 8-12x for bulk/commodity producers (MGPI)
- **Common pitfalls:** Ignoring aging inventory as a working capital drag on FCF; extrapolating premiumization trends that may slow; not adjusting for wine vintage variability; overpaying for "premiumization" narrative when volume is actually declining

## Turnaround Precedents

| Company | Period | Situation | Actions Taken | Outcome | Timeline | Stock Recovery |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------------|---------|----------|----------------|
| Constellation Brands wine/spirits | 2016-2020 | Declining wine/spirits segment (Svedka, Robert Mondavi mass-market lines), failed Canopy Growth bet | Divested low-end wine brands (~$1.7B to Gallo 2021), focused resources on premium beer (Modelo/Corona) | Success — wine drag eliminated, stock recovered on beer strength | 3-4 years | Business pivot rather than category turnaround |
| MGP Ingredients (MGPI) | 2015-2018 | Commodity whiskey distiller with low margins, private-label dependency | Acquired branded spirits (Remus, George Remus, Rossville Union), premium focus | Success — transformed from bulk to branded, margins expanded significantly | 3 years | ~$15 to $80+ |
| Brown-Forman (BF.B) | 2008-2011 | GFC-related volume decline, currency headwinds, premium spirits softness | Maintained brand investment, expanded internationally, Jack Daniel's line extensions (Honey, Fire) | Success — emerged stronger, consistent growth for decade | 3 years | ~$20 to $35 (split-adjusted) |

**Pattern:** 3 of 3 cases recovered -- ~100% base rate but small sample and survivorship-biased (no wine-only turnarounds found). Success required pivoting from commodity/bulk to premium branded, or divesting declining segments entirely. Heritage brands with pricing power (Jack Daniel's, George Remus) recovered well; companies stuck in oversupplied commodity wine did not.

## Risk Profile

- **Default risk type:** Operational / Cyclical
- **Cyclicality:** Low-Moderate — spirits more resilient than wine; premium segments more cyclical than value
- **Regulatory discretion:** Moderate — TTB licensing, state distribution laws (DTC shipping varies by state), potential for tariff impacts on imported spirits
- **Macro sensitivity:** Consumer income (Moderate — premium spirits correlate with discretionary spending), Trade policy/tariffs (High — EU/US tariffs historically impacted scotch, bourbon exports), Commodity costs (Moderate — grain, glass, cork)
- **Binary trigger frequency:** Rare-Occasional

### Kill Factors (hard reject)

1. **Forced liquidation of aging inventory** — if a distillery must sell aged whiskey at bulk prices to meet debt covenants, brand value destruction is irreversible
2. **Loss of core brand trademark or heritage** — brand equity is the entire business; loss of brand ownership or severe reputation damage
3. **Regulatory ban on core product category** — e.g., prohibition-style restriction (extremely unlikely but binary)

### Friction Factors (PCS modifiers)

1. **Trade war tariffs on spirits exports** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: EU tariffs on American whiskey historically cut export volumes 20%+ (2018-2021 tariff disputes)
2. **Wine oversupply / grape glut** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: California wine oversupply depresses pricing for years; structural oversupply hard to correct given 3-5 year vineyard lifecycles
3. **DTC regulatory restrictions tightening** — suggested friction: 0 to -1, rationale: state-by-state DTC shipping laws are unpredictable and restrict highest-margin channel

## Thesis-Breaking Patterns

- **Failure mode 1:** Wine oversupply destroying pricing — industry-wide grape glut collapses wine prices. Gradual. Examples: California wine glut 2019-2024 (bulk wine prices dropped 30-40%).
- **Failure mode 2:** Consumer preference shift away from category — spirits premiumization stalls or reverses. Gradual. Examples: vodka volume declining in favor of tequila/mezcal.
- **Failure mode 3:** Tariff escalation cutting export markets — retaliatory tariffs on bourbon/American whiskey. Binary trigger, gradual impact. Examples: EU 25% tariff on American whiskey (2018-2021).

## Evidence Requirements

### Must-Have Data Points

| Data Point | Where to Find It | Why It Matters |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Depletion vs shipment trends | 10-K MD&A, distributor reports | Channel inventory health (stuffing detection) |
| Price/mix by tier (premium vs value) | 10-K segment data, earnings calls | Premiumization validation |
| Aging inventory levels and costs | 10-K balance sheet, notes on inventory | Working capital quality and risk |
| DTC vs wholesale mix (wine) | 10-K segment reporting | Margin quality assessment |
| Export revenue and tariff exposure | 10-K geographic breakdown, risk factors | Trade policy vulnerability |
| Grape/grain cost trends | 10-K MD&A, commodity reports | Input cost trajectory |
| State DTC shipping law changes | Wine Institute, industry trade groups | DTC channel access monitoring |

### Red Flags If Missing

- If depletion/shipment data diverges (shipments >> depletions), channel stuffing likely
- If aging inventory costs are not disclosed, working capital risk cannot be properly assessed

## Epistemic Profile

| PCS Question | Typical Answer | Rationale |
|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| Q1: Operational risk? | Mostly | Core risk is execution, but tariff/trade policy adds non-operational dimension |
| Q2: Regulatory minimal? | Mostly | TTB licensing is stable but state DTC laws and tariffs introduce uncertainty |
| Q3: Historical precedent? | Moderate | Spirit brand turnarounds have precedent; wine-only turnarounds are rarer |
| Q4: Non-binary outcome? | Yes | Gradual brand decline or recovery; few truly binary events |
| Q5: Macro/geo limited? | No | Significant trade policy exposure (tariffs) and FX risk for exporters |

**Epistemic stability:** 3/5 -- Good business model understanding for spirits, but wine segment has oversupply uncertainty, trade policy creates unpredictable macro risk, and DTC regulatory landscape varies by state. Wine-only companies are harder to assess than diversified spirits companies.
**Suggested PCS friction:** -1 -- Trade policy and wine oversupply introduce cyclical/macro friction.
