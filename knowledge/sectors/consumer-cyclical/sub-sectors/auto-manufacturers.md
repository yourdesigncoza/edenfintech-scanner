# Auto Manufacturers

## Overview

Auto manufacturers (Ford, GM, Stellantis, Tesla, Rivian) design, build, and sell vehicles. Extremely capital-intensive with thin margins (EBIT 3-7% for traditional OEMs). Revenue is highly cyclical, tied to consumer confidence, credit availability, and interest rates. The industry is undergoing a massive EV transition requiring billions in retooling investment. Traditional OEMs face margin pressure from EV investments while legacy ICE profits fund the transition.

## Key Metrics & KPIs

| Metric | What It Measures | Healthy Range | Red Flag |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|----------|
| EBIT Margin | Operating profitability | 5-8% | <3% |
| Unit Sales Volume | Demand indicator | Stable or growing YoY | Declining 2+ quarters |
| Average Transaction Price (ATP) | Pricing power | Rising with inflation | Declining while costs rise |
| Inventory Days (dealer) | Demand/supply balance | 50-70 days | >90 days (overproduction) |
| Capex/Revenue | Investment intensity | 5-8% | >10% without revenue growth |
| Debt/EBITDA | Leverage | <2.5x (ex-financial arm) | >4x |
| Warranty Accruals | Quality indicator | Stable as % of revenue | Rising sharply |
| EV Mix % | Transition progress | Growing | Stagnant with heavy EV investment |
| Free Cash Flow | Cash generation | Positive after dividends | Negative for 2+ years |
| Market Share | Competitive position | Stable or growing | Losing >1% annually |
| Credit Portfolio Quality (captive finance) | Financial arm health | Delinquency <2% | Delinquency >3% |
| R&D/Revenue | Innovation investment | 4-6% | <3% (underinvesting) or >8% (burning cash) |

## Valuation Approach

- **Primary method:** P/E or EV/EBITDA. Traditional OEMs: 5-8x EV/EBITDA, 5-10x P/E. Tesla trades at massive premium (50-80x P/E) due to growth/tech narrative.
- **Why NOT P/FCF:** Auto manufacturers have highly lumpy capex cycles and captive finance arms that distort FCF.
- **Common pitfalls:** (1) Ignoring captive finance arm (GMAC, Ford Credit). (2) Applying tech multiples to legacy OEMs. (3) Not adjusting for pension/OPEB liabilities.

## Turnaround Precedents

| Company | Period | Situation | Actions Taken | Outcome | Timeline | Stock Recovery |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------------|---------|----------|----------------|
| GM | 2009-2013 | Chapter 11 bankruptcy, $172B liabilities | Government bailout, debt elimination, brand pruning (Pontiac, Saturn, Hummer) | Successful -- emerged profitable | IPO Nov 2010, profitable by 2012 | IPO at $33, recovered to $40+ by 2013 |
| Ford | 2006-2010 | Near-bankruptcy, $25B credit facility pledge of all assets | "One Ford" plan under Alan Mulally, sold Volvo/Jaguar/Land Rover, consolidated platforms | Successful -- avoided bankruptcy | 3-4 year turnaround | Stock from $1 (2008) to $18 (2011) |
| Chrysler/FCA | 2009-2014 | Chapter 11, government bailout | Fiat partnership, Jeep/RAM focus, eliminated unprofitable models | Successful -- merged into FCA then Stellantis | 5 years to full independence | Not directly comparable (private then IPO) |

**Pattern:** Auto manufacturer turnarounds require massive capital (usually government/strategic partner), brand simplification, and take 3-5 years. 3 of 3 major US cases recovered, but all required extraordinary intervention. Base rate misleading -- survivorship bias is extreme.

## Risk Profile

- **Default risk type:** Cyclical
- **Cyclicality:** Extreme
- **Regulatory discretion:** Moderate (emissions standards, safety recalls)
- **Macro sensitivity:** Interest rates: Extreme / Consumer confidence: High / Unemployment: High / Trade policy: High
- **Binary trigger frequency:** Rare but catastrophic (bankruptcy, massive recall)

### Kill Factors

1. **Negative equity with no government/strategic backstop** -- auto manufacturers cannot survive balance sheet distress without external capital
2. **Complete EV transition failure with legacy ICE in terminal decline** -- stranded assets become uninvestable

### Friction Factors

1. **EV transition execution risk** -- suggested friction: -1. Billions in investment with uncertain returns.
2. **UAW/labor relations disruption** -- suggested friction: -1. 2023 UAW strike cost $3.6B+ across Big Three.
