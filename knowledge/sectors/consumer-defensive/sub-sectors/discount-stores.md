# Discount Stores

## Overview

US discount retailers include Dollar General (DG, ~$38B revenue, ~20,000 stores), Dollar Tree (DLTR, ~$31B with Family Dollar, ~16,000 stores), Five Below (FIVE, ~$3.9B), Ollie's Bargain Outlet (OLLI, ~$2.3B), and Big Lots (BIG — filed Chapter 11 in 2024). The model is high-volume, small-format retail targeting low-to-moderate income consumers. Dollar stores generate revenue from consumables (food, cleaning — 75%+ of DG sales) and discretionary items.

Capital intensity is moderate. Dollar stores are cheap to build ($250K-$500K per new store) and generate fast payback (2-3 years). Gross margins: 30-35% (DG ~31%, DLTR ~36%). EBITDA margins: 8-12% (DG ~10%, DLTR ~8%). FCF is driven by high store count growth (DG opens 700-900 new stores/year) and low capex per store. The model relies on extreme operating efficiency — DG averages ~$1.8M revenue per store with 6-8 employees.

Barriers to entry are moderate: real estate access in rural/underserved areas, supply chain scale, vendor relationships. However, saturation risk is real — there are now ~38,000 dollar stores in the US (more than Walmart + Starbucks combined). Competition includes Walmart (Neighborhood Markets), Aldi, dollar stores competing with each other, and online retail for discretionary items.

## Valuation Approach

- **Primary method:** EV/EBITDA and P/E — standard for retail; unit economics per store critical
- **Secondary method:** Per-store valuation (EV / store count); DCF for growth modeling
- **Why range varies widely:** Growth stores (FIVE at 15-20x EV/EBITDA) vs mature operators (DG at 10-13x). Baseline FCF multiple 14-20x reflects mix of staples demand and retail execution risk.
- **Typical EV/EBITDA range:** 10-13x for DG/DLTR; 15-20x for FIVE (growth); 8-10x for struggling operators; 6-8x for distressed (BIG pre-bankruptcy)
- **Common pitfalls:** Extrapolating store count growth without adjusting for cannibalization; ignoring tariff impact on Chinese-sourced discretionary goods; treating same-store-sales during inflation as volume growth; underestimating labor cost inflation in a low-wage workforce

## Turnaround Precedents

| Company | Period | Situation | Actions Taken | Outcome | Timeline | Stock Recovery |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------------|---------|----------|----------------|
| Dollar Tree (DLTR) | 2019-2023 | Family Dollar acquisition (2015) integration failure, comp store declines, operational issues, activist pressure (Mantle Ridge) | New CEO Rick Dreiling (2022, former DG CEO), raised price point above $1, store renovations, closed 1,000 Family Dollar stores | Partial — improving but Family Dollar still underperforming | 3+ years | ~$80 to $145 (then back to ~$65 by 2025) |
| Dollar General (DG) | 2007-2009 | Overleveraged from KKR LBO ($6.9B, 2007), recession hit customer base, IPO timing | Operational excellence focus, strong same-store sales growth through recession (trade-down beneficiary), IPO 2009 | Success — deleveraged through growth, became premier dollar store | 2-3 years | IPO at $21 to $200+ over decade |
| 99 Cents Only Stores | 2022-2024 | Declining comps, margin compression, overexpansion, failed fixed-price-point model | Attempted price point increases, store closures | Failure — filed Chapter 11 (April 2024), liquidated all 371 stores | 2 years | Total loss |
| Big Lots (BIG) | 2022-2024 | Discretionary furniture/home focus hurt by post-COVID normalization, consumer pullback | Cost restructuring, store closures, real estate monetization | Failure — filed Chapter 11 (Sept 2024), acquired by Nexus Capital | 2 years | Total loss for equity |

**Pattern:** 1 of 4 achieved full recovery, 1 partial, 2 total failures -- ~25-50% base rate. Critical differentiator: consumable-focused dollar stores (DG) with operational discipline survived; discretionary-focused (BIG) or fixed-price-point (99 Cents Only) failed. Store saturation and tariff exposure are growing structural risks.

## Risk Profile

- **Default risk type:** Operational / Cyclical
- **Cyclicality:** Counter-cyclical for consumable dollar stores (benefit from trade-down); Cyclical for discretionary discount (Big Lots, Five Below)
- **Regulatory discretion:** Low — standard retail regulation (OSHA, CPSC, labor laws)
- **Macro sensitivity:** Consumer income/unemployment (Moderate — mixed; low-income customers are core but trade-down helps during recession), Tariffs on Chinese imports (HIGH — 40-60% of discretionary goods sourced from China), Labor costs/minimum wage (High), SNAP benefits (Moderate)
- **Binary trigger frequency:** Occasional — tariff shocks, store saturation, rapid deterioration in thin-margin businesses

### Kill Factors (hard reject)

1. **Tariff escalation making business model unviable** — if tariffs on Chinese goods reach 50%+ sustained, discretionary dollar store economics collapse (items cannot be repriced above psychological threshold)
2. **Store saturation cannibalization** — if new stores cannibalize existing store sales below breakeven. Already a concern with 38,000 US dollar stores.
3. **Fixed-price-point trap during inflation** — companies unable to raise prices above $1/$1.25 threshold while costs rise. Example: 99 Cents Only liquidation.

### Friction Factors (PCS modifiers)

1. **Tariff exposure on Chinese-sourced goods** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: 40-60% of discretionary merchandise sourced from China; tariff changes can immediately destroy category margins
2. **Store saturation risk** — suggested friction: -1, rationale: 38,000 dollar stores in US, cannibalization increasingly apparent in rural markets
3. **Labor cost inflation / minimum wage increases** — suggested friction: 0 to -1, rationale: low-wage workforce means minimum wage hikes have outsized margin impact
4. **Shrink/theft epidemic** — suggested friction: 0 to -1, rationale: organized retail crime disproportionately impacts dollar stores due to low staffing levels

## Thesis-Breaking Patterns

- **Failure mode 1:** Tariff-driven margin destruction — Chinese import tariffs make discretionary goods uneconomical. Gradual/Binary. Examples: 2018-2019 tariff impact on DLTR discretionary category.
- **Failure mode 2:** Saturation-driven comp decline — too many stores in same markets cannibalize each other. Gradual. Examples: Dollar General slowing comps in oversaturated rural markets (2023-2024).
- **Failure mode 3:** Failed acquisition integration — buying a struggling chain and failing to fix it. Gradual. Examples: DLTR/Family Dollar.
- **Failure mode 4:** Fixed-price-point bankruptcy — inflation destroys economics of $1 model. Binary. Examples: 99 Cents Only (2024).
